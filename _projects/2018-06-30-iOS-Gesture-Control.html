Designing Gesture Control for iOS

Redesign a phone interface using insights from individuals based on the everyday challenges they experience when interacting with their device

Human Computer Interaction

Johns Hopkins University

2019

with Alan Lai and Ride Bu

Problem Space

Smartphones have now become ubiquitous in most developed countries, enabling us access to processors more powerful than the first computers used for NASA’s moon landings at our fingertips. Though these advancements have made operating these devices intuitive and ready-to-hand, there are many instances where the seemingly natural controls appear to be a nuisance. The constraint of having to touch the screen has been very much enforced by the current interaction style, but with the recent advances in technologies, the potential for touchless gesture control is feasible, allowing for a new dimension of interaction between you and your smartphone.

Goal

Our project goal was to understand the pain points of iOS users in regards to their phone interactions and design relevant touchless, gesture controls for the iOS system. By integrating this with the iOS system itself, we hope to make seemleasly incorporate this new type of interaction style with an individual's workflow.

Cultural Probe Setup

In order to gain a deeper understanding of the fundamental interactions between someone and their iOS devices, we created and utilized a cultural probe to collect data. A cultural probe was done rather than contextual interviews since these inconvenient interactions happen sporadically and intermittently, making contextual inquiries unfeasible.

Our cultural probe consisted of two different parts. The first was an index card provided to participants that they used to jot down instances where it was inconvenient to use their phones. The second was a daily online questionnaire with the goal of getting more context and understanding of the fundamental issues and situations that led to these inconvenient events.



  • How many instances today was it physically inconvenient to touch your phone?

  • What were the settings/locations of the instances mentioned above?

  • What were the tasks that you wanted to accomplish during those instances?
  • What actions did you end up taking to interact with your device during those instances?

  • What sort of gestures, if any, do you think would help improve the accessibility of your phone

  • On a scale from 1 to 10, with 10 being very frustrated, how frustrated were you by the interactions with your phone interface today?

Findings

The participants of the cultural probe were primarily students, with varying daily activities such as working in a lab, playing a sport, and volunteering at a food bank. Inconvenient phone interactions that consistently came up occurred while participants were working with gloves, cooking, washing dishes, or taking a shower. Studies have shown that on average we pickup/check our phones anywhere from 100 to 150 times a day. With that in mind, our participants only reported frustration with a little under 10% of those interactions. However, those instances where it was difficult to interact with their phone often resulted in a disruption with their workflow or whatever they were doing at the time. Although the level of frustration varied greatly across our participants, most of them were receptive to motion gesture features and said they were very likely to try it.



“If we had some way of operating our phones without taking off our gloves, the lab workflow would improve immensely”




“Even simple features like waving my hand over the phone to unlock and show important information for the day would be helpful”



Persona



In order to capture some of the insights we gained from the people we worked with, we developed a persona to represent some of the overarching pain points we observed.

Storyboarding and Prototype

We developed some storyboards to demonstrate some scenarios where gesture control would be useful based on feedback from our participants.


Scenario 1: Demonstrates someone using the music playback gestures while taking a shower.


Scenario 2: Demonstrates someone working in the lab with gloves using motion gestures to set a timer.


Scenario 3: Demonstrates someone who is eating and watching YouTube, and uses motion gestures to pause the video and adjust the volume.



With our initial prototype we wanted to focus on designing gestures for apps/features that were commonly used among our cultural probe participants. We also wanted to provide functionality for gestures that we believed would be generally useful. Considering the difficulty of prototyping motion gestures without a working product, we decided to focus our brainstorming on intuitive motion gestures for some basic features.

Takeaways

From the cultural probe to the design process, this project provided valuable insights into the steps required and their importance in developing a prototype for an interaction style for a smart device.

Starting with the cultural probe, we quickly learned that our participants were quite similar. Although this was useful because we were able to solidify our findings for a certain group of people, it meant that our prototype may not be well suited to others. Ideally, we wanted more participants in the cultural probe and have them vary in terms of phone usage and background (age, major, etc.)

Additionally, we learned about the limitations of a video prototype. Our idea of iPhone motion gestures limited us to making a screen recording that synced hand motions with screen movements. Although it conveyed our idea to the participants quite well, they were unable to actually interact with the prototype. They could really only gauge the product based on one scenario we recorded.

Overall, the project was a very valuable learning experience. It was great to get exposure to running a culture probe and developing storyboards, and compare how these methods of gathering information and designing compared to other methods.
